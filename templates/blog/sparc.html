SPARC

Spatial Risk Calendar - World Food Programme

The general methodology for quantitative risk is based on following equation:

RS = PT * PL * V * A

PT is the temporal (e.g. annual) probability of occurrence of a specific hazard scenario (Hs) with a given return period in an area
PL is the locational or spatial probability of occurrence of a specific hazard scenario with a given return period in an area affecting the elements-at-risk
V is the physical vulnerability, specified as the degree of damage to a specific element-at-risk Es given the local intensity caused due to the occurrence of hazard scenario Hs
A is the quantification (value) of the specific type of element at risk evaluated. If the value is expressed in monetary terms, the risk may also be expressed as potential damage.
The overall analysis (depicted above) will aggregate data from different sources and in particular:
  • GAR15 global floodplain data (UNISDR)
  • population data (LandScan and WorldPop)
  • global precipitation data (FAO)
  • administrative boundaries (GAUL)
  • analysis of historical events (EM-DAT)
The vulnerability component (V) will use:
  •  land degradation component being developed as SPARC context layers
  •  people vulnerability based on socio-economic factors developed at VAM and in particular:
The Food Consumption Score (FCS), a tool developed by WFP, is commonly used as a proxy indicator for access to food. It is a weighted score based on dietary diversity, food frequency and the nutritional importance of food groups consumed.

The Coping Strategies Index (CSI) is an indicator of household food security. The CSI is used to measure the impact of food aid programmes, as an early warning indicator of impending food scarcity.

Flood hazard and people at risk


A hazard is a potentially damaging physical event, which has a probability of occurrence within a specified return period (temporal component) and within a given area (spatial component) and has a given intensity.
  • Spatial probability: probability of a hazard event with a certain intensity happening at a particular location (flood extent as a result of hydraulic modelling)
  • Temporal probability: probability that an event with a certain intensity happens within a given period (return period for each flood extent map as a result of hydraulic modelling)
  • Magnitude probability: probability that an event happening has a certain magnitude/intensity (flood depth as a result of hydraulic modelling)
The calculation has therefore been divided in two stages.
Once the spatial distribution of the hazard (where it could strike), characterised by its severity and frequency of occurrence (how often and how intense) is individuated it can then be used to assess the people exposed to the risk of flooding (number of people at risk).

This first step calculates the “Zonal Statistics” for each province contained in the list of countries aided by the WFP. The flood prone areas characterized by an annual probability are used to compute the number of people likely to reside in those areas. This analysis uses the cells in a raster that have the same value (return period in our case), whether or not they are contiguous, to summarize the total number of people living within the boundaries of the annual probability to be affected by flooding.
 
 
Additionally, using the flood zones and the (likely) position of the population (population figure or PF) is possible to multiply pixel-by-pixel to produce an expected impact value. This is central to SPARC, because it offers a monthly, pixel-level number of people who, based on historical frequency of hazard occurrence and most recent vulnerability data, can be incorporated into humanitarian planning. EIV uses the same logic as the expected value of a coin toss: if winning a coin toss is worth one dollar, and one has a 0.5 chance of guessing the winning side correctly, the expected value of each toss is 50 cents.
Thus in a simple SPARC example, if a pixel has a 0.1 chance of being flooded in any given March, and an estimated ARP of 1000, its March EIV is 100.

The second step, uses the ARP figures calculated on an annual basis and disaggregate the total value into a series of monthly values. This is achieved by calculating the percentage of annual rain falling in any month and applying the same probability to the annual figure of people at risk.
The monthly precipitation rate has been chosen because is known to be the most significant trigger for igniting the flood hazard.

Of all inputs considered, at least four of them are directly connected with precipitation and is the only parameter which varies monthly whilst other factors (such as land cover change) have in general much slower changes measurable in years.

Although some frameworks, including the European Flood Directive, advises that three flood hazards return periods are sufficient for estimation purposes, there is a wide consensus (Messner et al. 2007) that six return periods should be preferably used for estimating flood risk.
It is also known, that the most accurate estimates on damages and people at risk are achieved when the number of return periods are located in the lower tail of the risk curve, while adding an extra return period in the upper tail has very little influence on the final risk estimate. In this sense, the return periods provided by the GAR 2015 dataset represent a “quasi-optimal” dataset and could be to some extent misbalanced on the right side of the distribution (200, 500, 1000 compared to 25,50 using 100 years as the median of the distribution).
For this reason it could be important to check the availability of products about to be released by Bristol University that should include a 10 years return period or 10% annual probability with a total of annual probabilities of four.

Is important to recall that alterations to watercourses, such as channelization, change the validity of all historic data collected over the years, as well as the results of hydraulic modelling, therefore the boundaries of the flood prone areas could be quite different from the real ones. During the flooding of the Mississippi River in 1973 and 1993 water level rose to higher levels than expected from the statistical data, because modification of the stream had made the data invalid. The 1973 flood caused damage and rose to levels that might be expected from a 200-year flood, even though discharge was only a level predicted for a 30 years flood.

Due to the global nature of the GAR project, flood maps do not take into account flood defenses that are normally present to preserve the value exposed to floods. This can strongly influence the results of the risk calculations and especially of the economic parameters and creates a hiatus between the “coarse” scale of the flood data used and the detailed calculation required to assess risk at provincial level. This is especially relevant in countries where provinces are very small and would require a much more “realistic” flood extents but at the expense of the feasibility of the whole SPARC project.
The results of this is that the final calculation seems to overestimate the number of people at risk although in most cases the figures calculated are comparable with the outcomes of other studies and real flood events.

Monthly disaggregation of people at risk of flooding

Floods are usually caused by heavy or prolonged rainfall but can also occur due to landslides triggered by heavy rainfall or earthquakes, failure of dams or hydraulic structures, or high sea levels at river mouths.
Based on the commonly adopted “Source-Pathway-Receptor-Consequence” model, it is assumed that the source (precursor event) is rainfall and higher levels of precipitation are very likely to lead to high river discharge and consequently to flood events.
This approach, sometimes referred as “correlation with triggers”, has been successfully applied in landslide hazard analysis and is based on the assumption that the frequency of the landslides (or flood) is that of the triggering event.
This method, applied to floods, has a few drawbacks (e.g. different triggers can cause an event for example rainfall or snowmelt) yet seems a viable solution for establishing a simple procedure capable of disaggregating the annual probability into a monthly one.
In a future version the plan is to develop several correlations for different climatic regions using, for example, the monthly distribution of rainfall in regions where they are the main trigger of the floods and the data of snow cover in areas where flooding is due to snow-melt.
An additional improvement could use a stochastically generated rainfall based on the actual historical precipitation pattern. Recently, Thyer and Kuczera (1999) developed a hidden state Markov model to account for the wet and dry spells explicitly in annual rainfall.
In this preliminary phase, no attempt will be made to use any stochastically generated annual, monthly and daily precipitation but time and funds permitting, a Monte Carlo approach will be used to take into account year-to-year variations parameter where long periods of wet and dry years are observed.
In this early phase, however an attempt will be made to consider not only precipitation by adding additional factors (e.g. evapotranspiration) to build a more realistic scenario able perhaps to provide a more reliable short-term monthly estimates of flood occurrences likelihood.
For this purpose, several raster datasets were used, amongst those available at FAO geonetwork, for analysing different aspects of the flood phenomena according to the equation above.
The precipitation dataset chosen is a grid with estimated precipitation per month with a spatial resolution of 10 arc minutes. This dataset has been constructed from 27 075 stations with 1961-1990 climatological normals. The dataset consists of 12 ASCII-grids with mean monthly data in mm/day * 10, and one ASCII-grid with yearly data in mm/year. In addition, 12 ASCII-grid with monthly values at 5 arc minutes resolution are made available as input data for a global water balance model (GlobWat).

Evaporation is known to be the least of these quantities, particularly over short periods of time, but is the only data available together with precipitation on a monthly basis whereas infiltration and interception were assessed only partially as they should be derived from soil data and could easily require a study per se. Finally, the historical variability of precipitation has been used to see if all months are equally affected by high level of variability in terms of precipitation. Al the meteorological data have been compared with the historical occurrences related with flood, in order to evaluate any noticeable correlation.The result is visible in the next figure.
It seems evident that, as expected, the monthly precipitation presents a strong correlation with historical accidents and substantiate the choice to link the number of possible people affected month-by-month with the precipitation pattern. Evapotranspiration on the other hand, although showing a similar trend risk is not adding any significant improvement to the calculation using the rain pattern.
The variation of precipitation is probably also quite interesting showing that in months with more intense rainfall are also those with greater stability in the amount of precipitation, which instead is very high in the months with little precipitation.
This factor seems to be perfectly reasonable because, if there is little rain, a single heavy rain increases the spread between minimum and maximum values and therefore the variability. On the other hand we are comforted to know that the greatest error of estimate, in terms of population at risk, are to be expected to be found in months with minimal amount of precipitation and therefore with a lower level of risk.

Vulnerability

The people at risk of flooding need to be further screened assuming that not all people are affected to the same degree by natural hazards due to endogenous factors, such as water depth and velocity, as well as exogenous components such as the different level of resilience of different social classes and different ages.
Water depth and the velocity of water are important parameters, as possibilities for shelter decrease with increasing water depth with low-lying and densely populated areas, such as reclaimed areas or polders, will be most at risk. On the other hand, the global scale of our analysis and the lack of reliable data on dwelling at world level, reduce the actual possibility of using the information on the depth.
The vulnerability assessment is therefore entirely based on socio-economic and land-use derivative factors, as successfully experimented in the past (Peduzzi et al.) where five vulnerability parameters where used, namely:
·         GDP
·         purchasing power parity per capita,
·         modified percentage of arable land,
·         percentage of urban growth,
·         percentage of country forest coverage, transformed value of the percentage of the country dedicated to Crop land)
The above factors can be broadly divided in two main areas, with the last three items describing the intrinsic area vulnerability (AV) and the first two the people vulnerability (PV) in terms of copying capacity and economic capabilities.

In SPARC, a similar approach is used with the socio-economic parameters (AP) provided by Vulnerability Analysis and Mapping unit and detailed in the following table.
